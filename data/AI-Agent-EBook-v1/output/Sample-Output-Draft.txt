TITLE: Mastering AI Governance for Content Teams: A Practical Guide

CHAPTER 1: Why Content Teams Need an AI Governance Policy

Most content teams adopting AI tools skip governance entirely. They move fast,
get results, then hit a wall — a brand-damaging hallucination goes live, a
client's confidential data gets fed into a public model, or legal flags a
compliance issue nobody anticipated.

An AI governance policy isn't bureaucracy. It's the thing that lets you move
faster with confidence. Done right, it takes an afternoon to write and
prevents months of damage control.

This chapter shows you how to build version 1.0 of your content team's AI
governance policy — practical, usable, and finished by end of day.

THE FOUR THINGS YOUR POLICY MUST COVER

A minimal viable AI governance policy for a content team covers four areas:

1. Permitted use cases
   What can AI be used for? First drafts: yes. Client quotes: no.
   Data analysis: yes. Fabricating statistics: never.
   Be explicit. Ambiguity is where mistakes happen.

2. Data handling rules
   What data can be fed into AI tools? Internal strategy docs: probably not.
   Published research: yes. Client names in prompts: check your contract.
   Map your data sensitivity levels before you write a single prompt.

3. Output review requirements
   Which outputs need human review before going live? The answer should be:
   all of them, for now. As trust builds, you can tier your review process.

4. Incident response
   What happens when something goes wrong? Who gets notified? How fast?
   What's the process for correcting and retracting AI-generated content?
   Write this down before you need it.

COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

- Don't make it too long. A 40-page policy nobody reads is worse than no policy.
  Target: 2-3 pages maximum for version 1.0.
- Don't make it permanent. Schedule a quarterly review from day one.
- Don't write it in isolation. Include one person from legal, one from the
  team actually using the tools.

KEY TAKEAWAYS
- Write your AI governance policy before you scale AI adoption, not after.
- Cover four areas: permitted uses, data handling, review requirements, incident response.
- Keep version 1.0 to 2-3 pages. Iterate from there.
- Assign a named owner responsible for keeping it current.
- Review quarterly — AI tools and risks evolve faster than annual review cycles.
